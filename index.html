What is Archimedes Principle

Archimedes’ principle states that: “The upward buoyant force that is exerted on a body immersed in a fluid, whether partially or fully submerged, is equal to the weight of the fluid that the body displaces and acts in the upward direction at the center of mass of the displaced fluid”.

The value of thrust force is given by the Archimedes law which Archimedes of Syracuse of Greece discovered. When an object is partially or fully immersed in a liquid, the apparent loss of weight is equal to the weight of the liquid displaced by it.

Figure 1. Example of Archimedes Principle

If you look at the figure, the weight due to gravity is opposed by the thrust provided by the fluid. The object inside the liquid only feels the total force acting on it as the weight. Because the actual gravitational force is decreased by the liquid’s upthrust, the object feels as though its weight is reduced. The apparent weight is thus given by:

Apparent weight= Weight of object (in the air) – Thrust force (buoyancy)

Archimedes’ principle tells us that the weight loss is equal to the weight of liquid the object displaces.

The formula P = ρgΔh states that the pressure difference in a fluid is equal to the product of the fluid’s density (ρ), the acceleration due to gravity (g), and the change in height or depth (Δh). This means that pressure increases with depth in a fluid because the deeper you go, the more fluid there is above exerting weight. This relationship explains the basis of Archimedes’ Principle: the greater pressure at the bottom of a submerged object compared to the top creates an upward force known as the buoyant force, which makes objects float or sink depending on their density.

Archimedes' Principle Examples

Calculate the resulting force, if a steel ball of radius 6 cm is immersed in water.

Solution

Radius of steel ball = 6cm = 0.06m.
Volume of steel ball:
V = 4/3πr3
V = 4/3π0.063

∴V = 9.05 x 10-4m3

Density of water, p = 1000 kg.m-3 Acceleration due to gravity, g = 9.8 m.s-2 From Archimedes' principle formula,
Fb = p x g x V
Fb = (1000 kg.m-3)(9.8 m.s-2)(9.05 x 10-4 m3)
∴Fb= 8.87 N

What is Bernoulli's Principle

Bernoulli's principle states that for an inviscid flow, an increase in the speed of the fluid occurs simultaneously with a decrease in pressure or a decrease in the fluid's potential energy. In simpler terms, faster-moving fluids exert lower pressure, and slower-moving fluids exert higher pressure. This principle is a consequence of the conservation of energy and helps explain phenomena like how airplane wings generate lift and how a Venturi tube works.


The key aspect of Bernoulli's principle lies in:

  • Fluid speed and pressure:

    When the speed of a fluid (like air or water) increases, its pressure decreases, and vice versa.

  • Conservation of energy:

    The principle is derived from the conservation of energy principle, which states that the total energy (pressure, kinetic, and potential) in a fluid system remains constant along a streamline.

  • Application to fluids:

    This applies to various fluids, including liquids and gases.

Figure 1. Bernoulli’s-Equation Principle

Bernoulli's Principle Formula

From the diagram above, we can see the derivation setup for Bernoulli’s Equation, which describes the relationship between pressure, velocity, and height in a steady, incompressible fluid flow. When fluid moves from a wider area (point 1) to a narrower area (point 2) in a pipe of varying diameter and elevation, the pressure, velocity, and height are denoted as P1, v1, y1 and P2, v2, y2 respectively.

By applying the conservation of energy principle to the flow, we derive the famous Bernoulli’s Equation:

P1 + ½ ρv12 + ρgy1 = P2 + ½ ρv22 + ρgy2

Figure 2. Bernoulli’s-Equation Principle

Where:
P is the fluid pressure, ρ (rho) is the fluid density, v is the fluid velocity, y is the height above a reference point, and g is acceleration due to gravity.

Interpretation: This equation states that the sum of the pressure energy, kinetic energy per unit volume, and potential energy per unit volume is constant along any streamline. When velocity increases, pressure drops, and vice versa—an idea that underlies airplane lift, the working of carburetors, atomizers, and much more.
